# Research Question 1

## Q1: Does a structural gap exist between what current governance frameworks require at the decision level and what any published open specification provides?

<div class="answer-brief">
<p><strong>Answer in brief:</strong> Yes. Six governance frameworks examined — NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001, EU AI Act, ARAF v3.0, AIGN OS, and Oracle's OCI Agentic Governance Framework — all require accountability, traceability, and human oversight at the decision level. None specifies what a governed decision record contains. The gap is structural and consistent across all six. GDI is the first open specification to define the schema. This finding holds as of May 2026. <span class="conf-high">High Confidence</span></p>
</div>

---

### The Governance Altitude Problem

AI governance frameworks operate at three altitudes.

At the **organizational altitude**, frameworks govern roles, policies, risk registers, and board oversight. This layer is mature. NIST AI RMF's GOVERN function, ISO 42001's management system requirements, and the EU AI Act's operator obligations all operate here with well-developed guidance.[[1]](#cite1)[[2]](#cite2)[[3]](#cite3)

At the **system altitude**, model cards, bias audits, data lineage tools, and model registries govern how AI systems are built and validated. This layer is emerging. OneTrust, IBM OpenPages, Credo AI, and Holistic AI all operate here.

At the **decision altitude** — where a specific AI prediction meets a specific human and produces a consequential action — the governed artifact does not exist in any published open specification. This layer is absent.

Five structural reasons explain why this gap persists:[[4]](#cite4)

**IT governance inheritance.** Frameworks evolved from IT and data governance traditions that govern systems and data quality. Individual decisions were never the governed object.

**Static-dynamic mismatch.** Traditional compliance operates on quarterly or annual audit cycles. AI decisions occur at millisecond speed. Per-decision governance seemed architecturally infeasible until policy-as-code demonstrated inline enforcement was possible.

**Wrong abstraction layer.** The EU AI Act classifies AI systems by risk level, pushing governance to the system layer. Individual decisions within a high-risk system receive no independent governance treatment under the current text.

**Policy-proof gap.** An AI policy that says "humans remain accountable" is meaningless without an operational answer to: who had the right to delegate this action to the system, and can you demonstrate what happened.

**Governance as performance.** Only 24% of organizations have operationalized AI governance across their enterprise, even where policies exist.[[4]](#cite4)

### Framework-by-Framework Analysis

The following table presents a systematic comparison of six frameworks against GDI's GDR schema fields. Each entry is verified against published specification text, not vendor claims.

| Framework | Organizational | System | Decision | Decision-Level Specification |
|-----------|---------------|--------|----------|------------------------------|
| NIST AI RMF 1.0 | Present | Present | **Absent** | GOVERN names accountability structures. No decision record schema.[[1]](#cite1) |
| ISO/IEC 42001:2023 | Present | Present | **Absent** | A.6.2.8 requires event logs. No schema for individual decision artifacts.[[2]](#cite2) |
| EU AI Act 2024/1689 | Present | Present | **Partial** | Art. 12 logging, Art. 14 oversight, Art. 86 explanation — required but no implementation artifact specified.[[3]](#cite3) |
| ARAF v3.0 | Present | Present | **Partial** | Reconstructability principle named. Decision Supply Chain defined. No GDR schema provided.[[5]](#cite5) |
| AIGN OS | Present | Present | **Absent** | Layer 4 governance implementation required. No artifact schema specified.[[6]](#cite6) |
| Oracle OCI AGF (April 2026) | Present | Present | **Partial** | L1 Structured Decision Records capture policy enforcement outcomes. Pre-decision accountability fields absent.[[7]](#cite7) |

**GDI v3.0** provides schema-validated GDR fields across all required dimensions: options considered (minimum two, schema-enforced), evidence base with completeness classification, risk posture with explicit residual risk, accountability chain with named human owner, AI assistance disclosure, confidence zone, and downstream propagation map.

### The Oracle Distinction

Oracle's April 2026 OCI Agentic Governance Framework introduced Structured Decision Records and warrants precise treatment.[[7]](#cite7)

Oracle's SDR sits in the L1 evidence layer of a four-layer control architecture. It is produced *after* the Agent Runtime Controller has evaluated a proposed action against a policy pack and returned an enforcement outcome (ALLOW, DENY, REQUIRE\_REVIEW, ALLOW\_WITH\_REDACTION). The SDR records that the policy engine evaluated an action and reached a determination.

What the Oracle SDR does not record:

- The options the agent evaluated before proposing that action
- The evidence base on which the decision rested
- The named human who holds institutional accountability for decisions in this category
- The risk posture accepted and residual risk acknowledged
- The downstream dependencies that change if the decision changes

A compliance reviewer reading an Oracle SDR can answer: *was this action permitted by policy at execution time?*

A compliance reviewer reading a GDI GDR can answer: *who decided this category of action was appropriate, on what evidence, under what risk acceptance, and who holds accountability if the decision was wrong?*

EU AI Act Article 86 requires the ability to explain an individual decision to the person affected by it. Oracle's SDR provides a policy match record. GDI's GDR provides the decision rationale. The unit of governance differs: Oracle governs the action. GDI governs the decision.[[3]](#cite3)[[7]](#cite7)

---

### Mechanism

The gap persists because every framework governs either the *system* that makes predictions or the *organization* that deploys it. No framework governs the *moment* where a specific prediction becomes a human-authorized consequential action.

GDI's GDR closes this gap by treating the individual decision as a first-class governed artifact with schema enforcement, CI/CD validation, and accountability chain requirements. The mechanism is: every consequential decision must be captured in a schema-validated record *before the action executes*, containing the fields necessary to reconstruct the decision under regulatory scrutiny months or years later.[[8]](#cite8)

This mechanism is validated in the RGDS reference implementation (github.com/mj3b/rgds): 170+ commits, 7 releases, and a 114-test suite demonstrate that schema-validated decision governance is operationally feasible in a production CI/CD environment.[[9]](#cite9)

---

### Limitations

This analysis reflects a systematic search of published specifications, regulatory instruments, and vendor documentation conducted in May 2026. The field is moving fast. The Oracle OCI AGF was published in April 2026 — one month before this study. New specifications may close portions of the gap after this study's publication date.

The comparison table addresses the *specification* layer. It does not evaluate whether organizations implementing these frameworks have built decision-level artifacts outside of formal specification requirements. Some organizations may have internal decision record schemas. Those artifacts are not in the published specification literature and cannot be assessed here.

The "partial" ratings for EU AI Act and ARAF are accurate: both *require* decision-level governance without *specifying* the artifact. The partial rating acknowledges the demand while naming the missing implementation layer GDI provides.

---

### Implementation Guidance

For a practitioner assessing whether the gap claim applies to their organization:

**Question 1:** Can your organization produce, within 24 hours, a governed record of any individual AI-informed decision made in the last 12 months — containing the question decided, options evaluated, evidence consulted, risk posture accepted, and the named human who authorized the decision?

If the answer is no, the gap applies.

**Question 2:** If your organization was served an EU AI Act Article 86 request for explanation of an individual AI-informed decision, what artifact would you produce?

If the answer is a system-level audit log or model card, the gap applies. Article 86 requires explanation of the specific decision, not the system that produced it.

GDI's GDR is designed to answer both questions. The specification is available at [github.com/mj3b/governed-decision-intelligence](https://github.com/mj3b/governed-decision-intelligence) under Apache 2.0.
