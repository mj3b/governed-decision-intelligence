# Research Question 10

## Q10: Which regulatory instruments are most likely to create the first mandatory demand for GDI-class artifacts, and on what timeline?

<div class="answer-brief">
<p><strong>Answer in brief:</strong> Three regulatory instruments converge on a mandatory demand for decision-level governance artifacts. EU AI Act Article 86 creates enforceable demand starting August 2026. FDA algorithmic decision-making guidance is projected to mandate AI governance documentation as a submission element in 2027–28. SR 11-7 evolution is speculative but structurally probable in a 3–5 year window. The window of prior art advantage for organizations deploying GDI before these mandates take effect is approximately 12–24 months from May 2026. <span class="conf-med">Medium Confidence</span></p>
</div>

---

### Regulatory Trajectory Analysis

#### EU AI Act — High Confidence

The EU AI Act creates the most direct near-term demand. Article 86 creates a right for affected persons to obtain an explanation of individual decisions produced by high-risk AI systems. This right becomes enforceable for most high-risk systems by August 2026.[[1]](#cite1)

The Article does not specify what artifact satisfies the explanation requirement. It requires that the explanation be meaningful, individual, and accessible. A system-level audit log does not satisfy this requirement — it explains the system, not the individual decision. A GDI GDR contains the decision question, options evaluated, evidence base, and rationale. This is the information an Article 86 explanation requires.

Organizations that cannot produce a GDR-equivalent artifact for individual decisions made by high-risk AI systems face enforcement risk from August 2026. The enforcement mechanism is complaints to national supervisory authorities with fines up to €30M or 6% of global revenue.

#### FDA Algorithmic Decision-Making Guidance — Medium Confidence

FDA's January 2025 draft guidance requires documented human oversight of AI-assisted regulatory submissions.[[2]](#cite2) This is a voluntary guidance document, not a binding regulation. Its significance is as a signal of regulatory trajectory.

The RGDS independent study (Banasihan, 2026) projected a Phase 1 voluntary incentive structure (2026–27) transitioning to an anticipated Phase 2 mandate for AI governance documentation (2027–28), based on FDA's regulatory trajectory and international harmonization patterns.[[3]](#cite3) That projection remains the best available estimate. Medium confidence reflects the inherent uncertainty in regulatory timeline projection.

Organizations submitting INDs with AI-assisted components in 2026 that demonstrate GDI-equivalent documentation will be positioned ahead of the anticipated mandate and qualify for potential Phase 1 inspection advantages.

#### SR 11-7 Model Risk Management Evolution — Low Confidence

SR 11-7 has governed model validation in financial services since 2011.[[4]](#cite4) Its current scope covers model development, validation, and use at the system level. The logical extension as AI enters credit decisioning, fraud adjudication, and sanctions screening is per-decision accountability records.

ECOA already requires specific adverse action reasons for credit decisions regardless of whether a model or a human produced them.[[5]](#cite5) This requirement maps directly to a GDR's decision rationale field: the specific factors that influenced the adverse action are exactly what GDI's options considered and risk posture fields capture.

The evolution of SR 11-7 to per-decision accountability records is structurally probable but temporally uncertain. Low confidence reflects the absence of any published regulatory signal indicating this evolution is imminent.

---

### The Prior Art Advantage Window

GDI's Git commit history across `mj3b/governed-decision-intelligence` and `mj3b/rgds` provides date-stamped prior art for the decision-level governance architecture, the gate taxonomy, the confidence threshold model, and the AI assistance disclosure protocol.

Organizations that adopt GDI before regulatory mandates take effect gain three advantages. They avoid retroactive remediation — rebuilding governance infrastructure under enforcement pressure. They demonstrate governance maturity in audit and procurement contexts where that maturity is increasingly scrutinized. They contribute to a public specification that regulators may reference when developing implementation guidance, increasing the likelihood that GDI's architecture becomes the regulatory standard rather than a compliance response to it.

The window for this advantage is approximately 12–24 months from May 2026. EU AI Act Article 86 enforcement begins in August 2026. FDA Phase 2 guidance is projected for 2027–28. Organizations that wait until mandates are finalized will implement under pressure; organizations that adopt now will implement with the ability to shape interpretation.

---

### Limitations

Regulatory timeline projections are inherently uncertain. The EU AI Act Article 86 enforcement date is fixed; whether GDI's GDR specifically satisfies Article 86 requirements depends on regulatory interpretation not yet available. FDA Phase 2 guidance is projected, not announced. SR 11-7 evolution is speculative.

Confidence ratings are applied conservatively. High confidence for EU AI Act reflects the fixed enforcement date. Medium confidence for FDA reflects published trajectory evidence. Low confidence for SR 11-7 reflects structural probability without regulatory signal.
