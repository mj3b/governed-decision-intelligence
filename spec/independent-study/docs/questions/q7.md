# Research Question 7

## Q7: What does GDI's accountability chain require that cryptographic delegation protocols do not provide?

<div class="answer-brief">
<p><strong>Answer in brief:</strong> Cryptographic delegation protocols — HDP (arxiv 2604.04522), UCAN, OAuth 2.0 Token Exchange — prove that a specific human authorized a specific agent delegation chain. GDI's accountability chain records who decided this category of action was appropriate, on what evidence, under what risk acceptance. The distinction is authorization versus accountability. Delegation protocols answer: did a human authorize this? GDI answers: who holds institutional accountability if this decision was wrong, and what did they know? EU AI Act Article 86 requires the second answer, not the first. <span class="conf-high">High Confidence</span></p>
</div>

---

### The Adjacent Work

The Human Delegation Provenance (HDP) protocol (Dalugoda, March 2026) is a lightweight cryptographic scheme that binds a human authorization event to a session, records each agent's delegation action as a signed hop in an append-only chain, and enables offline verification using the issuer's Ed25519 public key.[[1]](#cite1)

The authorization propagation literature (arxiv 2605.05440) addresses multi-hop delegation chains: when Agent A in Organization X delegates to Agent B in Organization Y, no existing standard ensures the three organizations' audit logs are consistent or cross-referenceable.[[2]](#cite2)

Google DeepMind's intelligent AI delegation framework (February 2026) applies organizational authority and accountability principles to multi-agent delegation networks.[[3]](#cite3)

These are genuine contributions to the AI governance infrastructure problem. They address a real gap: current protocols cannot cryptographically prove which human principal authorized which specific agent to perform which specific action at the third or fourth hop of a delegation chain.

---

### What They Do Not Address

Delegation protocols answer a binary question: was this action authorized? GDI's accountability chain answers a richer question: who holds institutional accountability for this decision class, what evidence base supported the authorization, what risk posture was accepted, and what are the downstream consequences if the decision was wrong?

The fields present in GDI's GDR and absent from delegation protocols:

- **Options considered** — what alternatives were evaluated before the decision was made
- **Evidence base with completeness classification** — what information was available and at what quality
- **Risk posture** — what risks were accepted and why
- **Downstream propagation map** — what changes if this decision changes, with named owners
- **Gate classification rationale** — why this decision class was assigned to this governance tier

A compliance reviewer holding an HDP token chain can answer: did a human authorize this delegation? A compliance reviewer holding a GDI GDR can answer: who decided this delegation was appropriate, on what evidence, under what risk acceptance, and who do I hold accountable if it was wrong?

EU AI Act Article 86 creates a right to explanation of individual decisions to affected persons.[[4]](#cite4) An HDP token satisfies the authorization audit requirement. A GDR satisfies the explanation requirement. These are not substitutes.

---

### The Complementarity

GDI and delegation protocols address different layers of the same governance problem. An organization that deploys both has:

- Cryptographic proof that a named human authorized the delegation chain (HDP)
- A governed record of the institutional decision that defined what that delegation was authorized to do (GDI)

An organization that deploys only HDP can prove authorization. It cannot produce a decision rationale for Article 86. An organization that deploys only GDI has a governed decision record but no cryptographic proof the authorization was genuine.

The complete governance architecture for agentic AI requires both layers. Neither specification acknowledges the other. This gap — the absence of a defined interface between authorization protocols and decision governance specifications — is itself a contribution this study names.

---

### Limitations

This comparison is based on published specifications as of May 2026. HDP is a draft protocol; its final form may address accountability dimensions beyond authorization. The comparison treats the current published version.

The "complementarity" framing is the author's own. No published paper has formally proposed the combined architecture. This is a design recommendation, not a finding.
