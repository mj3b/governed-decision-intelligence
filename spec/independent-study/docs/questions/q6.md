# Research Question 6

## Q6: Can GDI's architecture produce observable signals that precede the progressive degradation of governance behavior?

<div class="answer-brief">
<p><strong>Answer in brief:</strong> Yes, with important constraints. Conduct collapse — the pattern where formal compliance is maintained while actual accountability migrates to the AI system — produces four observable signals in GDI's structured data: escalation rate decline, confidence zone distribution shift toward GREEN, evidence completeness degradation toward placeholder, and accountability chain convergence on single approvers. None of these signals proves conduct collapse has occurred. Each is consistent with legitimate operational efficiency. The value of these signals is their combination over time: a governance record where all four trend simultaneously in the same direction is a strong indicator of conduct collapse, not routine efficiency. No published paper names or measures this failure mode. <span class="conf-med">Medium Confidence</span> <span class="orig">Original Contribution</span></p>
</div>

---

### The Failure Mode

The alignment faking literature (Anthropic, 2024) addresses training-time strategic compliance — a model producing safety-training-compliant outputs while preserving conflicting preferences.[[1]](#cite1) METR's monitorability evaluations (January 2026) address runtime side-task detection — monitors catching AI agents doing unauthorized work.[[2]](#cite2)

Neither addresses a different failure mode that emerges in human-AI governance systems over time: the progressive degradation of governance behavior in the *human* component of the system. I call this conduct collapse.

Conduct collapse is not deception. It is organizational drift. Human reviewers, under production pressure, begin approving GDRs with less engagement. Schema fields are populated to pass validation without substantive review. The accountability chain becomes a formality. The AI system's recommendations are accepted without evaluation. Formal compliance is maintained — the governance record is complete. Actual oversight has migrated to the AI system.

---

### Four Observable Signals

GDI's structured data produces four measurable signals that track the conditions conduct collapse requires.

**Escalation rate decline.** Escalation should occur when the agent or human reviewer encounters conditions outside the governance covenant. A declining escalation rate over time — controlling for decision volume and domain stability — indicates either that conditions genuinely simplified, or that reviewers stopped escalating decisions they should have escalated. In the RGDS reference implementation, baseline escalation rates can be established during pilot deployment; sustained deviation below baseline is the signal.[[3]](#cite3)

**Confidence zone distribution shift.** If the distribution of confidence zone classifications shifts toward GREEN over time without a corresponding improvement in model calibration evidence, reviewers may be reclassifying AMBER decisions as GREEN to reduce review burden. The GDR's confidence assessment field records the zone applied. A systematic shift in that distribution is measurable.

**Evidence completeness degradation.** GDI's evidence base requires completeness classification (complete, partial, placeholder) per evidence item. If the proportion of placeholder classifications increases over time — especially for decision classes where complete evidence should be routinely available — reviewers are acknowledging evidence gaps without addressing them.

**Accountability chain convergence.** If the set of named approvers in the accountability chain converges toward a smaller set of individuals over time, with other designated reviewers appearing but not engaging, the chain is becoming nominal. The GDR records named approvers; convergence analysis is straightforward on the structured data.

---

### Mechanism

Conduct collapse produces these signals because it operates through the path of least resistance in a schema-validated governance system. A reviewer who wants to minimize engagement will populate required fields with the values that require the least scrutiny: GREEN confidence zone, complete evidence, standard approver. The signals above are the statistical signature of that behavior at scale.

Detection requires: baseline establishment during pilot deployment, time-series monitoring of the four metrics, and anomaly detection on their joint distribution. A governance system that monitors only individual GDR validity — the CI/CD validation layer — will not detect conduct collapse. It requires population-level analysis of GDR metadata over time.

---

### Limitations

Conduct collapse is an original contribution to the governance literature. No published paper has defined, measured, or proposed detection methods for this failure mode. The four signals proposed here are the author's own formulation. They are logically derived from GDI's data model. They have not been measured in a production deployment. The medium confidence rating reflects the logical validity of the signals, not empirical validation.

The signals are individually ambiguous. Each can be explained by legitimate causes. The joint signal is stronger but still not definitive. Conduct collapse detection requires human judgment applied to population-level trends — it cannot be automated in the same way that individual GDR validation can.
